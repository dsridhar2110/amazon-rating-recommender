Start here

What this project is for

The problem: a shop like Amazon has millions of products, and it wants to show each shopper the things they'll actually like. To do that, it needs to guess how a shopper would rate a product before they've bought it. The catch: most products have barely been rated — in my data the typical product was rated just once, and some products are brand new with no ratings at all. A simple system falls apart exactly there.

What I built: StarSense predicts the star rating (1–5) a shopper would give any product — even a product it has never seen before — and it explains which method it used and why. It's a recommendation engine: the same score that says "you'd give this 4.6" is what you'd use to rank and recommend.

Why it matters for me: it's my proof that I can take real, messy ratings data all the way to a product someone can click and use — with a model that stays honest and explainable. And it's the same shape as Siemens' service problem: predict a score for a huge catalogue of items where most are rarely seen, and hand a trustworthy, explained answer to a person — just with service parts and error codes instead of products.

The whole thing, simply

How I built it, from the start

If someone says "tell me about your project," this is the plain-English version — the technical words are in teal so I remember to actually say them.

1

The data and what I'm predicting. I started with about 745,000 real Amazon ratings — around 2,000 shoppers rating roughly 201,000 products (mostly movies and DVDs). Each row is "this shopper gave this product this many stars." The thing I'm predicting — the target variable — is that star rating, 1 to 5. The important quirk: the median product was rated only once, so most of the catalogue is barely known.

2

Cleaning and pre-processing. The data itself was tidy — no missing values — so the real work was preparing the product name, because the name is my main signal for brand-new items. I cleaned each name with Python and pandas: lower-cased it, stripped out noise like "(Special Edition)" or "(Blu-ray)", removed years, and standardised the spacing. Then I pre-computed statistics — each shopper's average rating and each product's average rating, with a confidence based on how many ratings back them.

3

The model. I built a hybrid recommender — three methods behind one smart switch (a router). If a product has enough ratings, it uses collaborative filtering (an algorithm called SVD) — "people who agreed with you before will probably agree again." If it's been rated only a handful of times, it uses a statistical blend of the shopper's habits and the product's average. And if it's never been seen, it reads the product's name with NLP (TF-IDF) to find similar products and borrow their ratings.

4

The extra — the cold-start part. The hard, interesting piece is that third method — the cold start. Most recommenders just give up on a brand-new item. Mine doesn't: because it can read the name, it still gives a sensible, explained answer for a product it has literally never encountered. And every prediction says which of the three methods fired and why — so it's never a black box.

5

The website. Finally I wrapped it all in a simple website and deployed it live — a page where you pick a shopper and a product and see the predicted rating with its explanation, plus a second page that shows how well the whole thing is performing. Anyone, technical or not, can click around.

Explain this to me further, in plain terms ▼

1 · "I hid a chunk of ratings"

It's like a practice exam where I already have the answer key. If the model studies all the ratings and I then quiz it on those same ratings, it can just memorise them and look perfect — which tells me nothing about new cases. So I hide about 15% (put the answers in a drawer), let it learn from the other 85%, then mark its guesses on the hidden ones. Now the score is honest. The jargon is a train/test split.

2 · "I measure the error with RMSE — about 0.81"

RMSE is just "on average, how far off is a guess, in stars." Lower is better.

If it guesses 4.2 and the truth was 5, it's off by 0.8. Do that across every hidden rating and the typical miss is my RMSE: 0.81 — usually within about eight-tenths of a star.

If they push: the "square" in RMSE punishes big misses more than small ones — being off by 2 stars hurts the score far more than being off by 0.5. That's on purpose.

3 · "I compare it to lazy baselines"

0.81 means nothing on its own — good compared to what? So I build the laziest guesses to beat: "just guess everyone's average" scores about 1.07; "guess this shopper's own average" about 0.98. Mine gets 0.81 — clearly better than both. That is what proves it learned real taste, not just the obvious.

4 · "I tested it two ways"

The clever bit — how I hide the ratings changes what I'm testing:

Realistic wayHide random ratings, but the shoppers are still known (only some products are new) — like real life, where customers are people we already have history on.0.81
Brutal wayHide whole shoppers, so every test shopper is a total stranger with no history — it can't personalise at all.1.08

The gap (1.08 − 0.81 ≈ 0.27) is a number for how much knowing a shopper is worth. A returning customer → ~0.81 error; a stranger → ~1.08. So a shopper's history is worth about 0.27 stars of accuracy — which turns a technical choice into a business sentence: "capturing customer history early is the highest-value thing we can do, and here's exactly how much."

Why an interviewer loves this

Most people report one number. I report two and explain why they differ — that shows I understand leakage and evaluation design. If they ask "which is the real number?": the 0.81 — in production every shopper is known; the 1.08 is a deliberate stress test.

What every word means

So I can explain the app, not just show it

Two lines on each term the app uses — what it is, in plain English, and why it's there.

What we've done the one-liner
Built a system that predicts the star rating a shopper would give a product — even products almost nobody has rated — and explains each guess. Same score powers recommendations.
Collaborative filtering SVD
The "people like you" method. It learns hidden taste patterns from everyone's past ratings — if you and I agreed on ten films, it bets we'll agree on the eleventh. SVD is the specific maths: it squeezes the giant shopper-by-product table into small "taste fingerprints" and multiplies them back to fill in a missing rating. I use it when a product has enough ratings to trust.
Statistical blend the middle case
For a product rated only a few times, there isn't enough for the taste engine. So I make a sensible guess by blending three things: how generous this shopper usually is, the product's own average, and similar products' averages — each weighted by how much data backs it (its confidence). More data → more say.
Content / NLP cold-start TF-IDF
For a product with no ratings at all, I read its name. NLP just means getting a computer to work with human text; TF-IDF is a classic way to turn a name into numbers by weighting the words that make it distinctive. I then find the closest-named known products and borrow their ratings. This is the cold-start tier — the hard, interesting part.
Cold start say this clearly
The problem of guessing for a brand-new item or shopper with no history. It's the make-or-break of any recommender: in my data the typical product has one rating, so cold-start isn't a rare edge case — it's most of the catalogue.
RMSE the score
"Root-mean-square error" — in plain terms, the typical size of the mistake, in stars. Lower is better. Mine is about 0.81, so a guess is usually within ~0.8 of a star of the truth. I use it because it's the standard, honest way to score a rating prediction.
Baseline the sanity check
The lazy version to beat — e.g. "just guess the global average," or "guess this shopper's average." If my proper model can't beat those, it hasn't earned its keep. Mine beats them all clearly, which proves it's actually learning something.
Strategy mix tier mix
What share of predictions each of the three methods handled. Here it's roughly 77% collaborative, 9% statistical, 14% cold-start. It shows the system leans on the strong method when it can, and only falls back when it must.
Calibration is it trustworthy?
"When the model says 4.5, is the real answer actually around 4.5?" Calibration checks that. I group predictions into buckets and compare the average predicted with the average actual in each bucket — if those two lines sit close, the scores mean what they say and a person can trust them.
Prediction distribution a sanity check
The spread of all the guesses — the average, the range, how many are low (under 3) versus high (over 4.5). Real Amazon ratings lean high (lots of 5s), so the guesses should lean high too. If they bunched up somewhere strange, that'd warn me something's off.

Explain data drift vs. model drift, in plain terms ▼

Two causes, one symptom. Model drift (or "decay") is just my model quietly getting worse in production over time. It has two possible causes:

Data drift — the inputs change

The data coming in starts to look different from what I trained on. Example: I trained on movie/DVD ratings, but the shop pivots to selling gadgets — now my cold-start tier is reading "Wireless Earbuds Pro Max" instead of film titles. The model works the same, but it's seeing a world it never learned.

Concept drift — the meaning changes

The inputs look the same, but the right answer changes. Example: a beloved film gets review-bombed after a controversy — same movie, but it now maps to 1 star instead of 5. The user→rating pattern I learned no longer holds.

How I'd catch each — and fix it

Data drift: watch whether the incoming products/ratings look different over time. Concept drift: watch my error and calibration on the monitoring page — if RMSE creeps from 0.81 up to 0.95, something drifted. Fix for both: re-train on fresh data, on a schedule or when drift crosses a threshold.

Siemens version: a new scanner model joins the fleet with unfamiliar readings = data drift; a firmware update changes what "healthy" looks like = concept drift.

Why each model landed where it did — two lines each:

SVD — the strongest single model 0.798
Matrix factorization that shrinks thin, noisy ratings toward the average, so it stays steady on a catalogue where most items are rated only once. That robustness is why it quietly beats even my shipped hybrid.
SVD-wide — same idea, more capacity 0.806
More factors and heavier regularization. Slightly worse alone (more room to over-fit sparse data), but it makes different mistakes than plain SVD — which is useful variety for the stack to blend.
KNNBaseline — "find similar users" 0.835
A neighbourhood model that copies people with similar taste. But with the median product rated only once, there aren't enough shared ratings to find reliable neighbours — so it's weaker than the factorization models.
NMF — factorization with a strict rule 1.050
Like SVD, but it forces every hidden factor to be positive. That constraint is too rigid for this noisy, 5-star-skewed data, so it barely beats just guessing the average.
LightGBM — why it lost to SVD 1.580
A tree model fed each item's raw average rating. But most items have only one rating, so those averages are unreliable and it over-trusts them, swinging wildly. SVD avoids this by mathematically shrinking shaky items toward the overall average — which is exactly why SVD wins.
Average of all five — why blending blindly hurt 0.870
A plain average drags the strong SVD down with the weak NMF and LightGBM. The lesson: averaging models blindly can be worse than just using your best single model.
STACKED — why the learned blend won 0.767
Instead of averaging, a small meta-model learns how much to trust each base depending on context — like how many ratings an item has. It leans on SVD, borrows useful scraps from the others, and ignores the junk — so it beats every single model.

The verdict I'd give: "My shipped model is 0.81 and fully explainable. I built a stacked ensemble that reached 0.767 — about a 5% improvement — but it's a less-explainable black box, so for a service team that has to trust the output I still ship the simpler one. Knowing the ceiling, and choosing not to chase it, was the point."

Two extras worth dropping: I also tried SVD++, but it was ~40× slower for a negligible gain, so I cut it — knowing when a heavier model isn't worth it. And I kept the test honest: the same held-out ratings for every model, and no vote-count features that wouldn't exist before a rating is even made (that would be leakage).

If I had more time

My roadmap — honest next steps, weakest part first

The live model is the explainable hybrid at 0.81. Here's exactly what I'd improve next — this is my answer to "what would you do with more time?". I start with the weakest part: cold-start, the ~14% of predictions for products with no rating history.

Making cold-start better — today it's a simple TF-IDF match on the product name, which only spots shared words. Better, strongest first:

1 · Semantic embeddings instead of TF-IDF biggest win
A transformer (Sentence-BERT) understands meaning, so "Space Western" and "Sci-fi Cowboy Epic" match even with zero shared words — which TF-IDF can't do. Directly targets the weakest tier. (This is the transformer work I'm saving for my NLP-classification project.)
2 · Use more than the name
Genre, category, description, cast, price. Cold-start is content-based filtering — the richer the content, the better the guess. Right now I only have the title, so I'm fighting one-handed.
3 · Predict the "taste fingerprint" from the text
Train a small model that maps an item's name → the hidden taste vector SVD would have learned, so a brand-new item gets an estimated collaborative profile instead of only borrowed averages.
4 · Personalise the borrow
Instead of borrowing similar items' average, learn what content this shopper likes from their history (e.g. they love sci-fi) and score the new item against their taste, not the crowd's.
5 · Smarter fallback + robustness
Shrink toward the item's category average rather than the global one, and use character-level matching so typos and title variants still find their match.

The accuracy ceiling — already tested. I built a stacked ensemble that reached 0.767 (~5% better — see "How far I pushed it"). It's tested and promising, but it's a black box, so I keep the explainable 0.81 live and hold the ensemble as a proven next step if accuracy ever became the priority.

The line I'd say: "The live model is explainable at 0.81. My roadmap, in order: semantic embeddings for cold-start, richer item features, then the stacked ensemble I've already validated at 0.767."

One thing worth knowing cold

Why "cold start" is the whole point

Cold start is the problem of making a good guess for a brand-new item or shopper with no history. It sounds like a rare edge case — it isn't. In my data the median product has been rated exactly once, and about 9% of the products I'm scored on were never seen in training at all. So handling cold-start isn't polish; it's most of the job.

The one line to drop: "A recommender that only works on popular items is easy and useless — the value is in the long tail. Mine handles the never-seen items by reading their names, and it maps directly to a service catalogue where the rare part or the new error code is exactly the one you can't afford to get wrong."

StarSense is an independent portfolio project (originally the FIT5212 recommender-systems assignment). Data is a remix of real Amazon product-review ratings. The model — a hybrid of SVD collaborative filtering, confidence- weighted statistics, and TF-IDF content similarity for cold-start — is a simple, honest baseline, not a production recommendation engine.
